2. Origin & Formation: From Quantum Foam to Living Planet

Cosmic Origins: From Quarks to Elements

The timeline of matter that would become Earth:

```
13.8 BYA: Big Bang
    ↓
Quark-gluon plasma → protons & neutrons (10⁻⁶ seconds)
    ↓
Primordial nucleosynthesis (3-20 minutes)
    ↓
75% Hydrogen, 25% Helium, trace Lithium
    ↓
First stars form (200-400 MY after Big Bang)
    ↓
Stellar fusion creates elements up to iron
    ↓
Supernovae create elements heavier than iron
    ↓
Multiple generations of stars enrich galaxy
    ↓
Solar nebula contains all elements (4.6 BYA)
    ↓
Earth forms from this enriched material
```

Stellar sources of Earth's elements:

Element Group Primary Source Examples
Hydrogen, Helium Big Bang H, He
Carbon to Iron Stellar fusion in massive stars C, O, Mg, Si, Fe
Elements > Iron Supernovae, neutron star mergers Au, U, Th, Pb
Lithium Cosmic ray spallation, some Big Bang Li

Formation of the Solar System

4.6 billion years ago: A giant molecular cloud in the Orion Arm begins collapsing:

· Trigger: Shockwave from nearby supernova (evidenced by aluminum-26 in meteorites)
· Gravitational collapse: Cloud fragments into dense core
· Conservation of angular momentum: Cloud spins faster, flattens into disk
· Protoplanetary disk: Central proto-Sun surrounded by gas and dust

Condensation Sequence in the Inner Solar System

Temperature gradient determined what materials condensed where:

Distance from Sun Temperature Condensed Materials
< 0.5 AU 1,300 K Refractory metals (Al, Ca, Ti oxides)
1 AU (Earth region) 500-1,300 K Silicates, iron-nickel metals
2-4 AU 150-500 K Water ice, carbon compounds
4 AU < 150 K Methane, ammonia ices

Accretion: Building a Planet

Stage 1: Dust to Planetesimals (0-1 million years)

· Dust particles collide and stick via electrostatic forces
· Grow to centimeter-sized pebbles
· Gravitational collapse forms kilometer-sized planetesimals
· Timescale: Incredibly rapid (10,000-100,000 years)

Stage 2: Runaway Growth (1-10 million years)

· Larger planetesimals gravitationally attract more material
· Growth accelerates (bigger = stronger gravity)
· Formation of Moon-to-Mars-sized "planetary embryos" (50-100 total)
· Timescale: ~100,000 years per embryo

Stage 3: Late-stage Accretion (10-100 million years)

· Giant impacts between embryos
· Final assembly of 4 terrestrial planets
· Earth's last giant impact: The Moon-forming impact
· Timescale: ~30-100 million years

The Moon-Forming Impact (The Giant Impact Hypothesis)

4.52 billion years ago: A Mars-sized protoplanet ("Theia") strikes proto-Earth:

· Impact angle: ~45 degrees, off-center
· Energy released: Equivalent to 100 million trillion Hiroshima bombs
· Effects:
  · Earth's core and Theia's core merge
  · Mantle material ejected into orbit
  · Earth's rotation rate set to ~5-hour day
  · Earth's axis tilted ~23.5°
· Resulting disk: Orbiting debris coalesces into Moon within ~100 years

Evidence for this event:

1. Moon's composition nearly identical to Earth's mantle
2. Moon's small iron core
3. Earth's rapid early rotation
4. Angular momentum of Earth-Moon system
5. Isotopic similarities between Earth and Moon

Differentiation: Core, Mantle, Crust Formation

4.5-4.4 billion years ago: The young Earth was molten (magma ocean):

· Heating sources:
  · Kinetic energy of impacts
  · Gravitational energy from core formation
  · Radioactive decay (short-lived isotopes like Al-26)
· Iron sank: Denser liquid iron droplets sink to center
  · This released gravitational energy (additional heating)
  · Core formed in <30 million years
· Silicates rose: Lighter minerals floated upward
  · Primordial crust formed
  · First atmosphere released (outgassing)

The Hadean Eon (4.5-4.0 billion years ago)

· Name: From Hades (Greek underworld) - hellish conditions
· Surface: Magma ocean gradually cooling
· Atmosphere: Thick CO₂, water vapor, nitrogen, toxic gases
· Impacts: Continuous heavy bombardment
· First crust: Mafic (basaltic) crust forms, recycled by impacts

When did the magma ocean solidify?

· Upper mantle: ~10-100 million years
· Global solidification: ~200 million years
· First stable crust: ~4.4 billion years ago (zircon crystals from Australia)

Origin of Water: The Great Debate

Where did Earth's water come from?

Source Contribution Evidence
Volcanic outgassing 10-50%? Water in mantle minerals
Carbonaceous chondrites 20-50% D/H ratio matches some meteorites
Comets <10% D/H ratio generally too high
Solar wind (hydrogen) Minor Creates water from oxygen in minerals

Current best estimate:

· ~50-70% from carbonaceous chondrites
· ~20-30% from mantle outgassing
· ~5-10% from comets

Timing of water delivery:

· Some water present during accretion
· Additional delivered during Late Heavy Bombardment (4.1-3.8 BYA)
· Continued slow delivery via asteroids

4. Internal Structure & Layers

From Core to Surface

```
                    ← Inner Core (solid Fe-Ni)
                 ← Outer Core (liquid Fe-Ni-S-O)
              ← Lower Mantle (perovskite + magnesiowüstite)
           ← Transition Zone (spinel structures)
        ← Upper Mantle (olivine, pyroxene, garnet)
     ← Crust (oceanic: basalt; continental: granite)
  ← Hydrosphere (oceans, ice, water)
← Atmosphere (N₂, O₂, trace gases)
← Magnetosphere (extending 10+ Earth radii)
```

1. Inner Core

Property Value
Radius 1,220 km (0.19 Earth radii)
Volume 7.6 × 10⁹ km³ (0.7% of Earth)
Mass ~1 × 10²³ kg (1.7% of Earth)
Composition Iron (85-90%), Nickel (5-10%), Light elements (<5%)
State Solid (despite extreme heat)
Temperature 5,200-6,000 K (5,000-5,700°C)
Pressure 330-360 GPa (3.3-3.6 million atm)
Density 12.8-13.1 g/cm³

Key facts:

· Solid due to immense pressure (prevents melting despite temperature)
· Growing at ~1 mm/year as outer core freezes
· Crystal structure: Hexagonal close-packed (hcp) iron
· Anisotropic: Different properties in different directions
· Rotation: May rotate slightly faster than mantle (super-rotation)

Discovery: Inge Lehmann (1936) from seismic waves

2. Outer Core

Property Value
Thickness 2,260 km (1,220-3,480 km radius)
Volume 1.69 × 10¹¹ km³ (15.6% of Earth)
Mass 1.85 × 10²⁴ kg (31% of Earth)
Composition Iron (85%), Nickel (5%), Light elements (~10% S, O, Si, C, H)
State Liquid
Temperature 4,000-5,500 K (top to bottom)
Pressure 135-330 GPa
Density 9.9-12.2 g/cm³
Viscosity Similar to water (surprisingly low)

Critical functions:

· Source of Earth's magnetic field: Convective motion + rotation = dynamo
· Flow velocity: ~10-20 km/year (slow by human standards, fast geologically)
· Patterns: Cylindrical rolls aligned with rotation axis

Light element debate:

· Sulfur is likely major component (lowers melting point)
· Oxygen, silicon, carbon, hydrogen also candidates
· Exact proportions determine core density and dynamics

3. Core-Mantle Boundary (D'' Layer)

Property Value
Depth 2,891 km
Thickness 200-300 km (variable)
Temperature jump ~1,000°C across boundary
Pressure 135 GPa

Complex region with:

· Ultra-low velocity zones: Partially molten patches (1-40 km thick)
· Chemical reactions: Core-mantle interaction (iron + silicates)
· Heat flow: Drives mantle plumes
· Anisotropy: Seismic waves travel differently in different directions

Seismic anomalies:

· Two large low-shear-velocity provinces (LLSVPs) beneath Africa and Pacific
· Possibly ancient, dense, chemically distinct material
· May be remnants of Moon-forming impact or primordial differentiation

Key features:

· Post-perovskite phase: Appears at ~2,700 km depth, changes properties
· Convection: Drives plate tectonics, moves heat upward
· Viscosity: Much higher than upper mantle (more resistant to flow)
· Heterogeneity: Not chemically uniform

Property Value

6. Upper Mantle

Property Value
Depth range Moho (5-70 km) to 410 km
Thickness ~340 km average
Composition Peridotite (olivine, pyroxene, garnet)
State Mostly solid, partially molten in asthenosphere

Sub-layers:

Lithospheric mantle (rigid):

· Depth: Moho to ~100-200 km
· Temperature: <1,300°C
· Coupled to crust (together form tectonic plates)

Asthenosphere (weak, partially molten):

· Depth: ~100-200 km to 410 km
· Temperature: 1,300-1,500°C
· 1-10% partial melt
· Low seismic velocity zone
· Allows plate motion

10. Magnetosphere

· Source: Dynamo in liquid outer core
· Shape: Teardrop (compressed sunward, elongated tail anti-sunward)
· Size: ~10 Earth radii toward Sun, extends past Moon's orbit in tail
· Bow shock: Where solar wind first meets magnetosphere (~90,000 km sunward)
· Van Allen belts: Trapped radiation (protons, electrons)
· Magnetopause: Boundary with solar wind (~65,000 km sunward)

11. Plate Tectonics

The Great Engine of Earth

Plate tectonics is unique to Earth in the Solar System—the process that:

· Recycles crust
· Builds mountains
· Triggers earthquakes and volcanoes
· Regulates climate via carbon cycle
· Creates and destroys ocean basins

Major Tectonic Plates

Plate Size (10⁶ km²) Type Major Features
Pacific 103 Oceanic Ring of Fire, Hawaii
North American 76 Mixed San Andreas, Rocky Mountains
Eurasian 68 Continental Alps, Himalayas (with Indian)
African 61 Mixed East African Rift
Antarctic 61 Mixed Southern Ocean
Indo-Australian 47 Mixed Himalayas, Indonesia
South American 43 Mixed Andes, Amazon

Plate Boundaries

Divergent (constructive) :

· Mid-ocean ridges (60,000 km total)
· New crust formed at 2-20 cm/year
· Example: Mid-Atlantic Ridge

Convergent (destructive) :

· Subduction zones (oceanic plates sink)
· Continental collision
· Examples: Andes (ocean-continent), Himalayas (continent-continent)

Transform (conservative) :

· Plates slide past
· Example: San Andreas Fault

Driving Forces

Force Contribution Description
Ridge push 5-10% Gravity sliding from elevated ridges
Slab pull 80-90% Dense subducting plate pulls the rest
Mantle drag 5-10% Convection currents drag plates

Wilson Cycle (supercontinent cycle)

· Every 400-600 million years, continents assemble and break apart
· Last supercontinent: Pangea (assembled 335 MYA, broke 175 MYA)
· Next supercontinent: Pangea Ultima or Amasia (~250 million years from now)

12. Magnetic Field & Magnetosphere

Field Properties

Property Value
Magnetic moment 7.79 × 10²² Am²
Surface field strength 25-65 μT (average ~45 μT)
Pole strength ~60 μT (poles), ~30 μT (equator)
Dipole tilt ~9.6° relative to rotation axis
Pole drift North pole moving ~40 km/year toward Siberia

Generation: Geodynamo

Requirements met by Earth:

1. Conducting fluid: Liquid iron in outer core
2. Convection: Thermal and compositional buoyancy
3. Rotation: Coriolis force organizes flow
4. Self-exciting: Motion + existing field generates current

Flow patterns:

· Helical motions due to Coriolis force
· Cylindrical rolls aligned with rotation axis
· Differential rotation (inner core spins faster)

Reversals

· Frequency: ~0.1-5 reversals per million years
· Last reversal: 780,000 years ago (Brunhes-Matuyama)
· Duration of reversal: 1,000-10,000 years
· Field during reversal: Weak, multipolar, multiple poles

Magnetosphere Functions

Function Benefit
Solar wind deflection Protects atmosphere from stripping
Cosmic ray shielding Reduces radiation at surface
Aurora creation Beautiful light shows
Navigation aid Animals use magnetic field

Auroras (Northern/Southern Lights)

· Source: Solar wind particles channeled to poles
· Altitude: 100-400 km
· Colors:
  · Green: Oxygen (most common)
  · Red: High-altitude oxygen
  · Blue/purple: Nitrogen
· Location: Auroral ovals (~20° from magnetic poles)

13. Weather & Climate

Atmospheric Circulation

Hadley Cells (0-30° latitude):

· Rising at equator (rainy, ITCZ)
· Descending at subtropics (deserts)
· Trade winds at surface

Ferrel Cells (30-60°):

· Mid-latitude circulation

19. Black Hole Possibility

Current State: NOT a Black Hole Candidate

Reason Explanation
Mass Too small by factor of millions
Density Not enough for gravitational collapse
Composition Normal matter supported by atomic forces
Fusion No nuclear reactions in core
Gravity Balanced by electron degeneracy pressure in atoms

Schwarzschild Radius Calculation

· If Earth collapsed: Rₛ = 2GM/c² = ~8.87 mm
· Current radius: 6,371 km (718 million × larger)
· Required compression: Impossible naturally

Theoretical Scenarios

· For black hole: Would need 3-5 solar masses minimum
· Artificial compression: Physically impossible with known physics
· Micro black holes: Could theoretically be created in particle accelerators (evaporate instantly)
· Primordial black holes: Earth-mass possible but hypothetical, would have been detected

If Earth Became Black Hole

· Same mass, same gravity at distance
· No light, no atmosphere, no surface
· Moon's orbit unchanged
· Life: Instant annihilation
· Appearance: Complete darkness where Earth was

21. Why Is Earth a Planet, Not a Star?

Planet Criteria (Earth meets all)

1. Orbits the Sun: Yes (primary criterion)
2. Sufficient mass for hydrostatic equilibrium: Yes (perfect sphere)
3. Cleared its orbit: Yes (dominates its region)
4. Not a satellite: Yes (orbits Sun directly)

Star Criteria (Earth fails all)

Star Criterion Earth's Status
Nuclear fusion No (core not hot/dense enough)
Mass threshold 0.08 solar masses needed (Earth has 0.000003 solar masses)
Self-luminous No (reflects sunlight, thermal emission from greenhouse)
Composition Rocky/metallic, not primarily H/He

Brown Dwarf Boundary

· Deuterium fusion: Needs 13 Jupiter masses (4,000× Earth's mass)
· Hydrogen fusion: Needs 80 Jupiter masses (24,000× Earth's mass)
· Earth: 0.0003% of brown dwarf mass
